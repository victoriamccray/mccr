# McCray Center for Community Research (MCCR)

> Community-governed research advancing patient agency through open science, participatory methods, and explainable technology.

**Status:** Draft Living Document

---

# 1. Vision

The McCray Center for Community Research (MCCR) is a community-governed research and technology institution that advances the collective learning, recovery, and agency of lived experience for chronic pain, repeated trauma, and other high-stress environments.

Through multimodal interpretation, MCCR is developing the Explainable Recovery Insights Model to help people understand behavioral, cognitive, physiological, and contextual signals within the context of their recovery. The community-governed learning system aims to enable participants to influence research priorities, algorithm refinement, governance, and knowledge production.

The institution is intended to close the gap between research, technology, and community action through open science, participatory research, workforce development, and trauma-informed services.

## 1.1 Identity

MCCR is a community-governed, public-interest translational research institution that develops explainable technologies, participatory research, and community programs to improve recovery agency for people living with chronic illness, trauma, and other high-stress environments.

# 2. Mission

Advance individual and community agency and strengthen systems of support for people living with chronic illness intersecting with trauma or other high-stress circumstances.

# 3. Theory of Change

MCCR is grounded in the observation that many of the populations we serve have experienced repeated uncertainty, adversity, and chronic stress. Research across neuroscience, psychology, and medicine suggests these experiences can alter pain processing, interoception, stress regulation, and prediction of internal bodily signals. We hypothesize that these changes contribute to reduced agency by making behavioral, physiological, and social signals more difficult to interpret accurately.

Repeated exposure to unpredictable environments may influence stress regulation, threat processing, interoception, and interpretation of bodily signals. MCCR investigates how supportive environments, improved signal interpretation, and community connection may restore adaptive agency.

Across healthcare and related sectors, substantial efforts have highlighted systemic barriers affecting the quality of medical care. Chronic pain is more often misattributed or misdiagnosed for vulnerable populations with lived experience of adverse developmental environments, substance use disorders, and autoimmune disorders that may be amplified by stress.

Research in cognitive neuroscience increasingly suggests that the ability to accurately predict internal pain-related signals may be diminished among people who have experienced trauma or severe mental health challenges. There is also substantial evidence that social, cognitive, and physical pain share overlapping neural and physiological architecture, which may contribute to increased pain intensity among people living with intersecting illnesses.

Additionally, there is extensive documentation describing the isolating experience of losing power and control while navigating chronic pain within healthcare systems. These experiences may be compounded by environmental stressors including loneliness, financial insecurity, and discrimination.

People living with chronic illness often navigate fragmented systems that affect access to care, quality of clinical interactions, social and emotional support, and the ability to advocate for holistic care across multiple providers.

People living with chronic illness, repeated trauma, and high-stress environments often experience reduced agency because the signals they receive from their bodies, minds, relationships, and institutions are complex, fragmented, and difficult to interpret. MCCR seeks to increase individual and community agency by making those signals more understandable through transparent technology, participatory research, peer support, and translational opportunities.

As individuals gain insight into their own recovery and communities collectively generate and govern knowledge, they become better equipped to navigate systems, advocate for themselves, and improve the systems that shape their health.

Many individuals served by MCCR have experienced disrupted caregiving systems, unstable environments, or periods where responsibility exceeded available support. These experiences may influence trust, prediction, self-efficacy, and the ability to access supportive resources. MCCR investigates how predictable environments, meaningful relationships, and interpretable information can strengthen adaptive agency.

# 4. Community Values

## Lived Experience

Lived experience is treated with equal value alongside scientific and methodological rigor to prioritize research that meaningfully affects the populations served.

## Participant Agency

Participants have the right and authority to govern, interpret, restrict, and maintain the privacy of their own data.

## Community Impact

Research affecting civil services, public infrastructure, healthcare, accessibility, equity, and community wellbeing should be conducted in service of supporting recovery across multiple dimensions.

## Reciprocity

Opportunities to collaborate and pay it forward with community members are integral to reducing barriers to connection, participation, and access to care.

## Open Science

Open-source software, open-access publishing, and open science practices should be preserved whenever feasible, except where doing so would compromise privacy, safety, or ethical responsibilities.

---

# 5. Governance

A Board of Directors should include balanced representation among researchers, technologists, individuals with lived experience, and clinical advisors.

An Executive Committee should include leadership responsible for finance, policy, operations, and organizational strategy.

MCCR will maintain checks and balances between executive operations (finance, policy, and administration) and programmatic implementation (technology, clinical activities, and research). Consensus-based decision making should be prioritized whenever feasible.

When disagreements arise between leadership groups, solution-oriented approaches emphasizing shared responsibility, collaboration, and transparency should be used.

---

# 6. Research Agenda

MCCR investigates how chronic illness, trauma, stress, and institutional systems influence recovery, agency, and wellbeing.

Current research priorities include:

- Translating opaque and complex systems into interpretable signals through participatory research.
- Developing and validating recovery assessment methods through participatory research with people who have lived experience.
- Investigating how repeated social and institutional stressors influence pain interpretation and behavioral adaptation.
- Developing explainable technologies that support recovery without replacing human judgment.

Priority populations include people experiencing chronic illness alongside housing insecurity, substance recovery, incarceration, disrupted education, immigration transitions, or other circumstances associated with limited access to stable support systems.

Current examples include analyses of health insurer privacy policies to examine how health information flows across third parties through consent and data-sharing mechanisms using deep learning-assisted analyses, and how those systems shape patient agency, particularly for vulnerable populations facing systemic barriers.

# 7. Technology Strategy

MCCR is developing a platform that combines behavioral, cognitive, and physiological signals collected through structured assessments and wearable devices to better understand an individual's recovery trajectory and generate explainable support alignment models for peer connection, recovery resources, and community learning.

The platform is designed to strengthen recovery by connecting people whose lived experiences, recovery patterns, and support styles are most likely to be mutually beneficial.

Technology developed by MCCR is intended to prioritize psychological safety, connectedness, and recovery rather than user engagement.

## 7.1 Technology Safety Principles

- Models do not diagnose or determine a person's identity.
- Participants control data-sharing permissions.
- Recommendations are suggestions, not prescriptions.
- Human oversight exists for high-risk situations.
- The platform prioritizes psychological safety over engagement.
- Participants should be able to understand why recommendations were generated.

# 12. Partnerships

MCCR prioritizes communities navigating chronic illness, trauma exposure, and high-stress transitions, including youth and young adults experiencing housing instability, formal or informal emancipation, disrupted caregiving environments, family estrangement, foster care transitions, incarceration impacts, substance recovery, educational disruption, immigration transitions, and other circumstances where stable support systems have been limited.

Potential partners include behavioral health organizations, chronic pain specialists, transitional housing providers, community mental health organizations, patient advocacy groups, universities, research institutions, nonprofit organizations, and organizations centered on lived experience.

# 13. Open Science

Compatibility code and core technical infrastructure involving participant data collection should be open source whenever possible without compromising privacy, safety, or ethical responsibilities.

Research findings should be disseminated for both scientific and community audiences using language that balances rigor with accessibility.

# 14. Five-Year Roadmap

### Year 1

Establish MCCR as an open, community-governed research institution by publishing the institutional charter, governance model, ethical AI principles, technology architecture, assessment framework, and research agenda through an open-source GitHub organization. Early software development prioritizes modular infrastructure, documentation, and reproducible research.

### Year 2

Develop community partnerships, establish a lived experience advisory council, and build participatory validation programs.

### Year 3

Expand feedback loops through fellowships, workshops, educational opportunities, and community engagement.

### Year 4

Strengthen outreach, research communication, fundraising, grant writing, and long-term sustainability while continuing to disseminate research findings.

### Year 5

Invest in organizational operations, leadership, staffing, and institutional processes while maintaining transparency and documenting organizational change.

# 15. Design Principles

## Agency over automation

Technology supports human judgment rather than replacing it.

## Transparency over certainty

Models communicate evidence, uncertainty, and alternative interpretations.

## Safety over engagement

Success is measured through recovery, trust, and community wellbeing rather than platform engagement.

## Community governance over unilateral control

People affected by the work have meaningful influence over research priorities, technology, and institutional policies.

## Scientific humility

MCCR distinguishes between established evidence, plausible interpretations, and active hypotheses, updating models as new evidence emerges.
