# McCray Center for Community Research (MCCR)

MCCR is a community-governed health technology and participatory research initiative dedicated to increasing self and community agency for people living with chronic illness, repeated trauma, and other high-stress life circumstances. We're building an interpretation layer that helps people understand complex behavioral, cognitive, and physiological signals so they can make more informed decisions about their health and recovery. By combining multimodal assessments with wearable data, MCCR generates explainable compatibility models for peer support, connecting people whose lived experiences, recovery patterns, and support styles are most likely to be mutually beneficial. In parallel, we're conducting AI-assisted research on health insurer privacy policies to examine how patient data flows through consent and third-party sharing, and how those systems shape patient agency for communities facing systemic barriers to care. Our long-term vision is to build community-governed infrastructure that combines community-centered support, explainable technology, and collective learning so people are active partners in understanding, improving, and governing the healthcare systems that affect them.

## The Research Framework

The referred pain hypothesis gives MCCR's community work a neurobiological grounding. Populations we serve are disproportionately people whose stress response systems were calibrated in high prediction error environments, whose pain has been displaced and misattributed, and who may carry inflammatory traces of relational trauma. The framework explains mechanistically why these populations present with the overlapping conditions they do. The center aims to empower leveraging evidence-based methods for neuroimmune plasticity and community-based care.

See `research/framework/` for working documents.

1 Vision

McCray Center for Community Research (MCCR) is a community-governed research and technology institution that advances the collective learning, recovery, and agency of lived experience for chronic pain, repeated trauma, and other high-stress environments. Through multimodal interpretation the explainable recovery insights model helps people understand behavioral, cognitive, physiological, and contextual signals in the context of their recovery. The community-governed learning system allows participants to influence research priorities, algorithm refinement, governance, and knowledge production. The translational institution is meant to close the gap between research, technology, and community action through open science, participatory research, workforce development, and trauma-informed services.

1.1. Identity

MCCR is a community-governed public-interest translational research institution that develops explainable technologies, participatory research, and community programs to improve recovery agency for people living with chronic illness, trauma, and other high-stress environments.



2 Mission

Advance self and community agency and systems support for people living with chronic illness intersected with trauma or high-stress circumstances.


3 Theory of Change

MCCR is grounded in the observation that many of the populations we serve have experienced repeated uncertainty, adversity, and chronic stress. Research across neuroscience, psychology, and medicine suggests these experiences can alter pain processing, interoception, stress regulation, and prediction of internal bodily signals. We hypothesize that these changes contribute to reduced agency by making behavioral, physiological, and social signals more difficult to interpret accurately.

Repeated exposure to unpredictable environments may influence stress regulation, threat processing, interoception, and interpretation of bodily signals. MCCR investigates how supportive environments, improved signal interpretation, and community connection may restore adaptive agency.

Across different sectors, there’s grown a substantial amount of efforts highlighting systematic barriers affecting the quality of medical care. Specifically, chronic pain is more often misattributed or misdiagnosed for vulnerable populations that have lived experience with adverse developmental environments, substance use disorders, and autoimmune disorders amplified by stress.

Specifically from cognitive neuroscience, there’s increasing evidence that the ability to accurately predict internal signals of pain is diminished for persons who’ve experienced trauma or severe mental health challenges. There is also substantial evidence that social, cognitive, and physical pain share underlying architecture in the body which leads to an increase in the experience and intensity of pain for people who have intersecting illnesses.

Additionally, there’s ample documentation about the isolating experience of loss of power and control for people who experience ongoing chronic pain during medical care. These things are amplified environmental stressors such as loneliness, financial security, and discrimination.

A common experience for folks living with chronic illnesses include navigating silos and systemic challenges from access to medical care, accuracy and quality of medical interactions with clinicians, social or emotional support, and advocating for holistic care among specialists.

People living with chronic illness, repeated trauma, and high-stress environments often experience reduced agency because the signals they receive from their bodies, minds, relationships, and institutions are complex, fragmented, and difficult to interpret. MCCR increases self and community agency by making those signals understandable through transparent technology, participatory research, peer support, and transitional opportunities. As individuals gain insight into their own recovery and communities collectively generate and govern knowledge, they become better equipped to navigate systems, advocate for themselves, and improve the systems that shape their health.

Many individuals served by MCCR have experienced disrupted caregiving systems, unstable environments, or periods where responsibility exceeded available support. These experiences may influence trust, prediction, self-efficacy, and the ability to access supportive resources. MCCR investigates how predictable environments, meaningful relationships, and interpretable information can strengthen adaptive agency.

4 Community Values

Lived experience is treated with equal value as scientific and methodological rigor in order to prioritize the impact of research affecting the populations served.

Users have the right and authority to govern, interpret, restrict, and remain private their own data. 

Insights that affect community staples including but not limited to civil services, public infrastructure, healthcare, accessibility and equity of community members and community-adjacent must be in service of supporting recovery holistically across modalities.

Opportunities to collaborate and pay it forward with community neighbors are integral to decomposing disabling barriers to connection and access to care.

Open-source, open access, and open science implementations are to be preserved whenever feasible except in cases where it poses a risk to privacy or safety to promote the reduction of barriers to educational resources.


5 Governance

A board of directors should include equal authority divided among researchers or scientists, technologists, lived experience, and clinical advisors.

An executive board committee shall have financial, policy and operations officers along with the CEO.

A system that enforces checks and balances between executive operations (finance, policy) and programmatic implementation (technology, clinical, research) will be upheld where consensus is required for decision making by vote.

In cases where decisions are halted by opposing executives, a solution-oriented approach will be employed where the sharing of power, workload and responsibilities are assessed collaboratively.



6 Research Agenda

We hypothesize that repeated social and institutional stressors may contribute to patterns of pain interpretation and behavioral adaptation analogous in some respects to referred pain in somatic systems. MCCR seeks to investigate whether these mechanisms can help explain why chronic illness, trauma, and social adversity often co-occur and reinforce one another.

Translating opaque and complex systems into interpretable signals is a primary aim of participatory research.

Clinical algorithms for assessing recovery are validated by members with lived experience.

Adverse circumstances support includes populations for unhoused or housing-insecure youth (16-22). previously incarcerated individuals, recovering from substance use, no completed high school or nontraditional undergraduate degree, and immigrants or refugees.

Examples include health insurer privacy policies to examine how health information flows across third parties through consent and data-sharing mechanisms using deep learning-assisted analyses, and how those systems shape patient agency, particularly for vulnerable populations facing systematic barriers.


7 Technology Strategy

The platform combines behavioral, cognitive, and physiological signals collected through structured assessments and wearable devices to better understand an individual's recovery trajectory and generate explainable support alignment models for peer connection, recovery resources, and community learning. The model is designed to strengthen recovery by connecting people whose lived experiences, recovery patterns, and support styles are most likely to be mutually beneficial. The platform is not meant to optimize based on user engagement and should prioritize psychological safety, connectedness, and monitor for recovery-related events.


7.1 Technology Safety Principles

Models do not diagnose or determine a person's identity.

Participants control data sharing permissions.

Recommendations are suggestions, not prescriptions.

Human oversight exists for high-risk situations.

The platform prioritizes psychological safety over engagement.

Participants can understand why recommendations were generated.

13 Open Science

The compatibility code and core technical platform code that involves user data collection should be open source without compromising user privacy or ethics policies.

Research findings should be disseminated for an open science educational audiences and available with a balance rigor and plain language prioritized for community learning.



14 Five-Year Roadmap

Year 1 focuses on building the core peer support and intelligence platform. That includes partnering with computational scientists and researchers for infrastructure to be hosted on an open source framework such as GitHub along with an accessible online presence. Additionally, publishing research related to theoretical questions and analyses around complex and opaque systems as well as mechanisms for support.

Year 2 focuses on engaging community partners to build out validation programs of the software. This includes building a council for lived experience and community partners as well as establishing participatory methods and protocols.

Year 3 involves building feedback loops for the pay it forward model and opportunities for previous research participants and partners to stay connected via events, fellowships, and educational workshops.

Year 4 involves more outreach and research communication strategies to build long term sustainability. This includes fundraising, impact reporting, grant writing as well as maintaining dissemination of research findings for action across scales.

Year 5 involves investing inward for staff, operations, leadership, and processes. Transparency and visibility of methods or systems remain critical as the organization grows and changes, all change management should be documented.



15 Design Principles

Agency over automation. Technology supports human judgment rather than replacing it.

Transparency over certainty. Models communicate evidence, uncertainty, and alternative interpretations.

Safety over engagement. Success is measured by recovery and trust, not time spent on the platform.

Community governance over unilateral control. People affected by the work have meaningful influence over research priorities, technology, and policies.

Scientific humility. MCCR distinguishes between established evidence, plausible interpretations, and active hypotheses, and updates its models as new evidence emerges.
